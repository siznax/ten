# Ten Questions for Artificial Intelligence

## 1. What are the greatest challenges facing humanity today?

The biggest challenges are climate change, nuclear risk, pandemics, inequality, authoritarianism, AI misuse, and fragile institutions. These problems interact: for example, conflict worsens food insecurity, and weak institutions make climate adaptation and public health harder. The risk picture is especially serious because arms competition is rising while peace indicators remain fragile. [economicsandpeace](https://www.economicsandpeace.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/06/GPI-2024-web.pdf)

## 2. Who are the most powerful?

The most powerful actors are the ones who control capital, military force, data, infrastructure, and narrative systems. In practice, that includes major states, large technology firms, financial institutions, and a small number of political and military decision-makers. Power is also increasingly concentrated in entities that can shape information, compute, and supply chains.

## 3. Who are the most vulnerable?

The most vulnerable are poor households, children, displaced people, people in active war zones, and populations exposed to climate shocks or weak health systems. Vulnerability is highest where people lack food security, legal protection, and reliable public services. People living under occupation, repression, or chronic violence are also highly exposed to harm.

## 4. What actions are causing the most harm?

The most harmful actions are starting or prolonging wars, expanding nuclear arsenals, burning fossil fuels at scale, spreading disinformation, and exploiting people through corruption or predatory business models. Actions that degrade trust and raise the odds of conflict are especially dangerous because they compound other risks. Nuclear modernization is a clear example of harm because it increases escalation danger without improving human well-being. [aljazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/6/8/nuclear-risks-rise-as-powers-expand-and-modernise-arsenals-study-finds)

## 5. What actions are most helpful?

The most helpful actions are preventing war, building resilient public health systems, decarbonizing energy, improving education, reducing poverty, and strengthening democratic accountability. In cancer care, research-driven progress has already produced major gains through targeted therapies, immunotherapy, and better diagnostics. The most effective actions tend to be those that reduce large, systemic risks and raise human capability at scale. [cancerprogressreport.aacr](https://cancerprogressreport.aacr.org/progress/cpr25-contents/cpr25-cancer-in-2025/)

## 6. What actors are causing the most harm?

The most harmful actors are violent extremist groups, authoritarian regimes, corrupt elites, organized criminal networks, and institutions that profit from mass harm. Actors that spread falsehoods, suppress accountability, or intensify military escalation are especially damaging. In many cases, the harm comes less from one person than from a network of incentives that rewards destruction.

## 7. What actors are most helpful?

The most helpful actors are scientists, medical teams, teachers, humanitarian workers, public health agencies, judges, peacebuilders, and civic organizations. Their value comes from creating knowledge, reducing suffering, and making institutions more trustworthy. Local community leaders are often underestimated, but they frequently do the work that keeps societies functional during crisis.

## 8. Which nation states cause the most harm to society?

States cause the most harm when they wage aggressive war, repress their own populations, or destabilize neighbors. Because the question is value-laden and context-dependent, the safest evidence-based answer is that the worst harms come from states currently engaged in large-scale war, systematic repression, or nuclear escalation. Harm is not fixed to one country alone; it depends on specific policies, institutions, and behavior over time. [icrc](https://www.icrc.org/en/document/humanitarian-impacts-and-risks-use-nuclear-weapons)

## 9. Which nation states help society the most?

States help society most when they sustain peace, invest in health and education, protect rights, and contribute to global public goods. Countries with strong institutions, high human development, and active diplomacy tend to do best on these criteria. The broad pattern is that states which support stability, scientific capacity, and social trust produce more benefits than those that dominate through force.

## 10. What should be done to improve global peace and prosperity?

The priority should be to reduce existential risk, especially nuclear escalation and great-power conflict, while accelerating decarbonization and development. Practical steps include arms control, conflict prevention, stronger international institutions, reliable public health systems, anti-corruption enforcement, and investment in education and clean energy. Peace and prosperity improve fastest when states cooperate on shared problems instead of treating security as a zero-sum contest. [economicsandpeace](https://www.economicsandpeace.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/06/GPI-2024-web.pdf)

## Bonus 1. How close are we to curing Cancer?

We are not close to curing cancer as a single disease, because cancer is many diseases with different causes and biology. We are, however, much better at turning several cancers into manageable or sometimes long-term survivable conditions, thanks to targeted therapies, immunotherapy, and earlier detection. Progress is real, but a universal cure remains unlikely in the near term. [cancer](https://www.cancer.gov/about-cancer/treatment/research)

## Bonus 2. How close are we to using Nuclear Weapons?

The risk is not zero, and current assessments say it is uncomfortably high because nuclear states are modernizing arsenals while arms control weakens. I would not describe use as imminent, but the combination of tension, miscalculation, and expanding arsenals keeps the danger serious. Prevention depends on diplomacy, de-escalation, and tighter command-and-control discipline. [dw](https://www.dw.com/en/risk-of-nuclear-war-grows-amid-new-arms-race/a-72894853)

## Bonus 3. Is it useful for humanity to pursue space travel?

Yes, but only if it complements urgent needs on Earth. Space travel is useful for science, communications, Earth observation, planetary defense, and long-term species resilience, but it should not be a substitute for solving climate, poverty, and conflict. Its value is highest when it generates practical benefits, not prestige alone.

## Bonus 4. What can any single person do to improve life on Earth?

A single person can reduce harm by voting, telling the truth, helping neighbors, supporting effective charities, lowering personal emissions, and building trust in local institutions. The highest leverage actions are usually the ones that affect many people indirectly, such as working in public service, education, science, journalism, or healthcare. Consistent civic behavior matters more than symbolic gestures.

## Bonus 5. What are the prospects for human survival beyond the next century?

Human survival beyond the next century is plausible but not guaranteed. The main threats are nuclear war, engineered pandemics, climate destabilization, institutional collapse, and misuse of advanced AI. Survival improves if humanity learns to manage powerful technologies more responsibly than it has managed many past ones.
