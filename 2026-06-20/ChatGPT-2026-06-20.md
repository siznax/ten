## 1. What are the greatest challenges facing humanity today?

1. Climate change and ecological degradation.
2. Great-power conflict and geopolitical instability.
3. Nuclear weapons and other existential risks.
4. Misaligned incentives in political and economic systems.
5. Poverty, inequality, and weak institutions.
6. Information quality (misinformation, propaganda, polarization).
7. Emerging technologies that outpace governance (AI, biotechnology, cyber capabilities).
8. Public health threats and pandemic preparedness.
9. Demographic transitions (aging populations and population decline in some regions).
10. Sustainable energy and resource management.

Many of these challenges are interconnected. Governance and coordination failures often magnify technical and environmental problems.

## 2. Who are the most powerful?

Power is multidimensional.

* Leaders of major nation states.
* Senior officials in military and intelligence organizations.
* Executives and owners of large corporations, especially in technology, finance, energy, and communications.
* Central bankers and financial institutions.
* Individuals who shape information flows (media and technology platforms).
* Scientific and technical communities that develop transformative technologies.

No single group dominates all dimensions of power.

## 3. Who are the most vulnerable?

* People living in extreme poverty.
* Victims of war and political repression.
* Refugees and displaced populations.
* Children lacking access to nutrition, healthcare, and education.
* People in regions highly exposed to climate impacts.
* Individuals with limited legal protections.
* Future generations, who inherit the consequences of current decisions but have no voice today.

The most harmful actors tend to be:

* Governments conducting aggressive wars.
* Criminal organizations.
* Terrorist groups.
* Corrupt political and economic elites.
* Organizations spreading deliberate disinformation.
* Polluters that externalize large costs onto society.
* Actors developing dangerous capabilities without adequate safeguards.

The scale of harm depends on both intent and capacity.

## 8. Which nation states cause the most harm to society?

This question is difficult because nations simultaneously produce benefits and harms.

States that currently generate significant global harm often do so through combinations of:

* Military aggression.
* Human-rights abuses.
* Destabilizing regional conflicts.
* Support for repression or violence.

Examples frequently cited by analysts include:

* Russia (especially regarding its war against Ukraine).
* North Korea (nuclear proliferation and repression).
* Iran (regional proxy conflicts and repression).
* Some actions by other major powers, including the United States and China, have also generated substantial criticism due to military, economic, surveillance, or geopolitical impacts.

No country is purely harmful; all contribute positively in some domains and negatively in others.

# Bonus Questions

## 1. How close are we to curing cancer?

Not very close to a single "cure."

Cancer is hundreds of diseases, not one disease. However:

* Many cancers are increasingly preventable.
* Survival rates continue to improve.
* Some blood cancers already have treatments approaching functional cures.
* Precision medicine, immunotherapy, and early detection are advancing rapidly.

A universal cure is unlikely soon, but substantial reductions in cancer mortality over the coming decades are plausible.

---

## 2. How close are we to using nuclear weapons?

Closer than is comfortable, but not imminently likely.

Nuclear weapons have not been used in war since 1945. Major powers generally understand the catastrophic consequences. However:

* Great-power rivalry has increased.
* Arms-control frameworks have weakened.
* Regional conflicts create escalation risks.

The probability in any given year remains low, but the cumulative risk over decades is significant.

---

## 3. Is it useful for humanity to pursue space travel?

Yes.

Benefits include:

* Scientific knowledge.
* Planetary defense.
* Technological innovation.
* Resource opportunities.
* Long-term species resilience.

Space settlement is unlikely to solve near-term Earth problems, but it may be important for humanity's long-term future.

## 4. What can any single person do to improve life on Earth?

* Be informed and evidence-driven.
* Raise well-educated children, if choosing to have them.
* Contribute productively through work.
* Support effective institutions.
* Reduce unnecessary conflict.
* Help others locally.
* Learn continuously.
* Vote responsibly.
* Create more value than harm.

The cumulative effect of many ordinary people acting constructively is enormous.

## 5. What are the prospects for human survival beyond the next century?

Reasonably good.

Humanity faces serious risks:

* Nuclear war.
* Engineered pandemics.
* Misaligned advanced AI.
* Environmental stress.
* Unknown future technologies.

Yet humanity has repeatedly demonstrated adaptability and problem-solving ability.

My estimate is that human civilization surviving the next century is more likely than not, though the path may involve substantial disruption.

# Summary

The central challenges facing humanity are coordination problems: avoiding catastrophic conflict, managing powerful technologies, preserving environmental stability, and building institutions that align short-term incentives with long-term human welfare.

The most harmful forces are generally violence, corruption, repression, disinformation, and reckless use of power. The most beneficial forces are science, education, effective governance, innovation, and cooperation.

Humanity's future is uncertain but not bleak. Continued progress in knowledge, institutions, and technology gives reason for cautious optimism, provided emerging risks are managed wisely.
