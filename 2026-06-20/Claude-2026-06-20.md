## Question 1: What are the greatest challenges facing humanity today?

**1. Climate Change and Ecological Breakdown**

The destabilization of Earth's climate system remains the defining long-term challenge. Rising temperatures, extreme weather events, sea-level rise, ocean acidification, and mass species extinction are accelerating. Feedback loops — permafrost thaw, ice-albedo loss — risk making these changes self-reinforcing beyond any human intervention.

**2. Nuclear and Catastrophic Weapons Risk**

The world has not eliminated nuclear weapons and the number of nuclear-armed states has grown. Geopolitical tensions between major powers have increased the risk of miscalculation or deliberate use to levels not seen since the Cold War.

**3. Artificial Intelligence Alignment and Governance**

AI is developing faster than governance frameworks. The risks include: AI systems optimizing for misspecified goals, AI-enabled authoritarian control, autonomous weapons, large-scale disinformation, and — over longer horizons — systems with capabilities beyond human oversight.

**4. Global Inequality and Poverty**

Billions of people lack adequate nutrition, healthcare, clean water, and economic opportunity. Inequality within and between nations has grown. This generates social instability, migration pressure, conflict, and denies humanity the full potential of billions of minds.

**5. Pandemic and Biosecurity Risk**

COVID-19 demonstrated the catastrophic vulnerability of global society to pandemic pathogens. Advances in biotechnology have simultaneously improved medicine and lowered the barrier to engineering dangerous pathogens — a dual-use risk that is not adequately governed.

**6. Democratic Erosion and Geopolitical Fragmentation**

Liberal democratic institutions are under strain worldwide. Authoritarianism is resurgent. International cooperation frameworks — the UN, WTO, arms control treaties — are weakened precisely when humanity needs them most to address collective problems.

**7. Resource Scarcity and Food Security**

Freshwater stress, soil degradation, and climate disruption to agricultural systems threaten the food security of billions, particularly in the Global South.

**8. Mental Health and Social Cohesion**

A global mental health crisis — loneliness, anxiety, depression, addiction — is undermining human flourishing in wealthy and developing nations alike. Social media ecosystems appear to be worsening this, particularly among young people.

# Bonus Questions

## Bonus 1: How close are we to curing cancer?

We are not close to a single "cure for cancer" — because cancer is not one disease. It is hundreds of distinct diseases with different causes, mechanisms, and optimal treatments.

However, remarkable progress is underway:

- **Immunotherapy** (checkpoint inhibitors, CAR-T cell therapy) has produced durable remissions in cancers once considered uniformly fatal, including some leukemias, melanomas, and lung cancers.
- **Targeted therapies** — drugs matched to specific genetic mutations — have transformed treatment of breast cancer (HER2+), lung cancer (EGFR), and leukemia (BCR-ABL).
- **mRNA cancer vaccines** — building on COVID-19 mRNA technology — are in late-stage trials for melanoma, pancreatic cancer, and others.
- **Early detection** — liquid biopsies detecting circulating tumor DNA may enable cancer detection years before symptoms, when treatment is most effective.
- **AI-assisted diagnosis** — improving accuracy of radiology and pathology.

Realistic outlook: Within the next 10–20 years, many common cancers will likely become manageable chronic conditions rather than death sentences. Childhood cancers and some blood cancers already have very high cure rates. Pancreatic, glioblastoma, and advanced solid tumors remain very difficult. A universal cure is not imminent, but survivorship is improving dramatically.

## Bonus 2: How close are we to using nuclear weapons?

Closer than at any time since the Cuban Missile Crisis. This is not hyperbole.

**Risk factors:**

- Russia's repeated implicit nuclear threats during the Ukraine war, and the explicit revision of its nuclear doctrine to lower the threshold for use.
- North Korea's continued expansion of its nuclear arsenal and delivery systems.
- Pakistan-India tensions with both nations nuclear-armed.
- Iran's nuclear program advancing.
- Erosion of arms control treaties (INF treaty gone; New START in limbo).
- Reduced direct communication channels between major nuclear powers.
- AI and cyber vulnerabilities in nuclear command-and-control systems.

**Mitigating factors:**

- No nuclear weapon has been used in war since 1945, establishing a powerful normative taboo.
- All nuclear states appear to understand mutual assured destruction logic.
- Intelligence services maintain active crisis monitoring and back-channel communications.
- Civil society and diplomatic institutions actively work to prevent use.

The Bulletin of Atomic Scientists' Doomsday Clock — a rough but respected expert judgment — has in recent years stood at 90 seconds to midnight, the closest it has ever been. That does not mean use is likely in the near term, but the risk is not negligible. Even a 1–2% annual probability of nuclear exchange is deeply alarming over a 50-year horizon.

## Bonus 3: Is space travel useful for humanity?

Yes — with important qualifications about priority and equity.

**Arguments in favor:**

- **Long-run survival insurance** — a single-planet civilization is existentially vulnerable to asteroid impact, supervolcanic eruption, runaway climate change, or nuclear war. Off-world backup is rational for civilizational risk management.
- **Scientific knowledge** — space exploration has generated fundamental advances in astronomy, planetary science, physics, and Earth observation (climate monitoring).
- **Spinoff technology** — GPS, satellite communications, weather forecasting, medical imaging technologies all trace roots to space programs.
- **Inspiration and international cooperation** — ISS represents one of the most sustained examples of international scientific cooperation in history.
- **Resource access** — asteroid mining could theoretically relieve terrestrial resource constraints, though this is speculative and distant.

**Arguments for caution:**

- The costs of human spaceflight are extraordinary; robotic missions deliver far more science per dollar.
- Private space ventures risk creating new arenas of geopolitical competition and billionaire prestige projects.
- Acute global suffering — poverty, climate, disease — could arguably claim priority.

**Conclusion:** Space investment at a moderate scale is justified both scientifically and strategically. It should not come at the expense of urgent terrestrial needs, but is not zero-sum with them in the context of major economies.

Honestly: uncertain, but not hopeless. The factors are genuine and competing.

**Reasons for hope:**

**Projection:** Civilizational collapse within a century is possible but not probable if current trends in energy transition, AI safety, and international governance continue to improve. The century after next presents greater uncertainty — civilizational survival depends on decisions made in the next 30–50 years. The window for effective action is open, but closing.
