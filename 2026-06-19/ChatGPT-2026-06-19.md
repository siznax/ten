# 1. What are the greatest challenges facing humanity today?

The greatest challenges are interconnected systemic risks rather than isolated problems.

### 1. Climate and ecological destabilization

Climate change, biodiversity loss, freshwater depletion, soil degradation, ocean acidification, and pollution threaten food systems, infrastructure, migration stability, and long-term economic productivity. These risks compound over decades and disproportionately affect poorer populations.

### 2. Governance failure in a technologically accelerated world

Human institutions evolve slowly while technologies evolve rapidly. Artificial intelligence, biotechnology, cyberwarfare, surveillance systems, and autonomous weapons create capabilities that governments and societies may not be prepared to regulate responsibly.

### 3. Nuclear weapons and great-power conflict

Large-scale war between nuclear powers remains among the few near-term threats capable of causing civilizational collapse. Escalation risks are amplified by cyberattacks, misinformation, and automated military systems.

### 4. Inequality and institutional distrust

Extreme wealth concentration, declining trust in institutions, corruption, disinformation, and political polarization weaken democratic governance and social cohesion.

### 5. Global public health vulnerabilities

Pandemics remain a major threat due to dense urbanization, global travel, antimicrobial resistance, and uneven healthcare access.

### 6. Cognitive and informational degradation

Algorithmic media systems optimize for engagement rather than truth. Misinformation, propaganda, and outrage-driven media ecosystems impair collective decision-making.

### 7. Long-term existential risks

Potential existential threats include misaligned advanced AI, engineered pandemics, runaway climate effects, and nuclear war.

# 2. Who are the most powerful?

Power is multidimensional: military, economic, technological, informational, institutional, and cultural.

### Most powerful categories

1. Major nation states
   Especially the United States and China due to economic scale, military capability, technological influence, and control over global systems.

2. Large technology companies
   Corporations controlling AI, cloud infrastructure, semiconductors, search, social media, and communication systems increasingly shape public discourse and economic activity.

3. Financial institutions and capital allocators
   Central banks, sovereign wealth funds, major investment firms, and reserve-currency issuers influence global markets and political outcomes.

4. Information platforms and media networks
   Actors capable of influencing narratives at planetary scale possess enormous soft power.

5. Scientific and engineering elites
   Small groups designing AI systems, semiconductor supply chains, biotechnology platforms, and energy systems increasingly affect humanity’s future trajectory.

Power is increasingly concentrated in organizations that control computation, data, energy, finance, and military infrastructure.

# Bonus 1. How close are we to curing cancer?

“Cancer” is not one disease but hundreds.

Humanity is unlikely to discover a single universal cure soon. However:

* Survival rates for many cancers have improved dramatically.
* Immunotherapy, targeted therapies, AI-assisted drug discovery, and early detection are advancing rapidly.
* Some cancers may become manageable chronic diseases.

Progress is substantial but uneven. A complete “cure for cancer” is unlikely in the near term; major reductions in mortality are realistic.

# Bonus 2. How close are we to using nuclear weapons?

Uncomfortably close.

Since 1945, nuclear deterrence has prevented large-scale use, but risks remain significant due to:

* Great-power rivalry
* Regional conflicts
* Cyber vulnerabilities
* Human error
* Escalation dynamics

The probability in any given year may be low, but cumulative long-term risk is substantial.
