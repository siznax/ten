## Bonus Questions

### 1. How close are we to curing Cancer?

We are not close to a single, monolithic "cure" because cancer is hundreds of distinct diseases defined by diverse genetic mutations. However, in 2026, we are closer than ever to turning cancer into a manageable chronic condition.

* The five-year relative survival rate in the U.S. has hit a historic 70%.
* **Immunotherapies** (like checkpoint inhibitors, T-cell engagers, and personalized CAR-T therapies) are rewriting survival curves for advanced, metastatic melanomas and lung cancers.
* Emerging techniques like **Regulated Induced Proximity Targeting Chimeras (RIPTACs)** and **Targeted Protein Degradation (TPD)** are entering advanced clinical phases to force specific cancer cells to self-destruct without destroying healthy tissue.

### 2. How close are we to using Nuclear Weapons?

The risk of nuclear weapon use is at its highest point since the end of the Cold War. The collapse of the rules-based international order, the expiration of bilateral arms control treaties like New START, and the growth of active warheads (exceeding 9,700 available for use globally) have created an unstable environment. Vertically expanding arsenals in Asia, active nuclear saber-rattling in Europe, and the integration of AI into early-warning systems elevate the risk of escalation through miscalculation or cyber sabotage. While immediate use remains low due to Mutually Assured Destruction, the systemic margin for error has narrowed dangerously.

### 3. Is it useful for humanity to pursue space travel?

Yes, it is highly useful. The utility of space travel is dual-pronged:

* **Terrestrial Spinoffs:** Technologies developed for space survival—advanced water purification, lightweight materials, resilient photovoltaics, and satellite-based Earth monitoring—are critical to solving climate and resource challenges on Earth.
* **Existential Redundancy:** In the long term, distributing human civilization across multiple celestial bodies mitigates the risk of single-planet extinction events (e.g., asteroid impacts or unmitigated biowarfare).

### 4. What can any single person do to improve life on Earth?

An individual can maximize their positive impact through **Effective Altruism**:

* **Optimize Philanthropy:** Direct disposable income to highly vetted, transparent charities that treat preventable diseases or combat extreme poverty (e.g., purchasing malaria bed nets).
* **Engage in Epistemic Hygiene:** Refuse to share unverified, emotionally manipulative information online, thereby slowing down the degradation of public discourse.
* **Reduce High-Impact Consumption:** Adopt a more plant-centric diet and minimize personal carbon footprints.

### 5. What are the prospects for human survival beyond the next century?

The prospects are highly favorable, but conditional. Humanity possesses the technological capacity, wealth, and scientific understanding to thrive indefinitely. However, our survival depends entirely on narrowing the **"wisdom gap"**—the delta between our god-like technological power (AI, biotech, nuclear) and our tribal, short-sighted socio-political institutions. If we survive the critical transition period of aligning frontier technologies and stabilizing global carbon systems over the next 50 years, the long-term survival prospects of the species are exceedingly high.
